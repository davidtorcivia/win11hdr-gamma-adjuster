Gloam

Fix the gray that Windows HDR casts over everything. Then let your screen follow the sun down.

Gloam repairs Windows 11's broken SDR-in-HDR gamma, per monitor, in pure math, with nothing to install. And it replaces Night Light with a night mode that gets the color science right: blended in linear light, scheduled by the real sun, composed cleanly with your calibration. When you want proof, a colorimeter takes the same pipeline to a measured, verified, HDR-native calibration.

The reason Gloam exists

Windows lifts your HDR shadows. Gloam puts them back.

With HDR enabled, Windows 11 decodes every SDR window using the piecewise sRGB curve. Almost nothing you look at was mastered for that curve: three decades of games, film, and the desktop itself were tuned on gamma 2.2 displays. The mismatch lands exactly where your eyes are most sensitive, pouring extra light into the shadows and turning blacks into a flat gray haze.

Gloam rebuilds the tone curve inside the HDR signal path itself: it undoes the sRGB assumption and redoes the decode as gamma 2.2 or BT.1886 gamma 2.4, independently per monitor. Pure math. No colorimeter, no profiles to manage, no dependencies.

  • Per monitor, on a hotkey. Gamma 2.2, 2.4, or Windows default, switchable globally without opening a menu.
  • Self-healing. A ramp guard notices when a game or driver resets the correction and silently restores it within seconds.
  • Native apply. Sub-millisecond writes straight to the GPU gamma ramp, no helper process.
  • HDR headroom respected. Highlights above SDR white blend smoothly to passthrough, so specular peaks stay bright.
After sundown

A night mode with the math done right.

Warming a screen is a color transform, and most tools get the color wrong. Windows Night Light applies its filter at the compositor, where it stacks unpredictably with anything else touching the display pipeline. Gloam computes the warm shift in linear light, where light physically mixes, and bakes it into the same high-precision pipeline as your gamma and calibration before anything is quantized.

It follows the real sun, too: sunset and sunrise are computed for your location, with smooth fades, per-app exclusions, and per-monitor temperature offsets so a warm evening still looks matched across displays. It works on SDR monitors as well as HDR.

  • Linear-light blending. Temperature shifts are applied where light actually adds, not in gamma-encoded space.
  • Planckian-locus accuracy. A CIE 1931 mode places the white point exactly on the blackbody curve at every temperature.
  • Real sunset and sunrise. Computed from the sun's position at your location, not a fixed clock time.
  • Composes with calibration. Warming is baked into the same LUTs as your correction, so nothing flickers, bands, or fights.
With a colorimeter

Go further: calibration that proves itself.

Everything above runs on Gloam's own native pipeline with zero dependencies. Add a colorimeter and the same pipeline becomes a full display calibrator: ArgyllCMS is downloaded automatically just to drive the meter, and the correction is installed as a native Windows MHC2 profile and verified by measurement.

HDR-native, wire-exact

Corrections install as a native MHC2 color profile applied by the Windows compositor itself, persistently, in HDR and SDR. HDR runs measure true HDR-range patches through a DirectX FP16 surface at exact PQ wire positions, far above SDR white.

Hands-free measurement

Connect an X-Rite i1 Display or any ArgyllCMS-supported meter and Gloam runs the whole flow: measure, build the correction, apply it, and re-measure through the applied profile to prove it worked.

Verification in depth

Native vs. calibrated dE2000 with histograms, worst and best patches, grayscale and primaries breakdowns. HDR verifications add a PQ tracking sweep graded in absolute nits against ST.2084 and dE ITP (BT.2124).

Reports and history

Every calibration produces a scored report kept in history, so you can track a panel as it ages, with an eyes-on A/B toggle and printer-friendly PDF export.

Meter spectral corrections

An in-app browser for the DisplayCAL community corrections database (.ccss/.ccmx) matches your meter to your panel's emission spectra. Essential on QD-OLED and wide-gamut displays.

Multi-monitor dashboard

A grid view of every connected display with real EDID names, HDR/SDR status, current gamma mode, and quick access to settings. Everything persists per monitor and restores on startup.

Before you ask

Questions

Do I need a colorimeter at all?

No. The core feature that brought most users here, fixing Windows 11's washed-out SDR-in-HDR shadows with proper gamma 2.2/2.4 decoding, is pure math and needs no hardware. Night mode, per-monitor white point trims, and the dashboard all work without a meter. A colorimeter is only required for measurement-based calibration and verification.

How is the night mode better than Windows Night Light?

Three ways. First, the color math: Gloam blends color temperature in linear light, where light physically mixes, and its accurate CIE 1931 mode keeps the white point exactly on the Planckian locus as the screen warms. Night Light is a compositor-level filter with none of that discipline.

Second, composition: Night Light fights external gamma corrections for control of the pipeline. Gloam bakes the warm shift into the same high-precision LUTs as your gamma preference and calibration before quantization, so the result is band-free and conflict-free.

Third, scheduling: sunset and sunrise are computed from the sun's actual position at your location, with configurable fades, per-app exclusions, and per-monitor temperature offsets. Disable Night Light, let Gloam own the pipeline, and the war is over.

Does it work in HDR? In SDR?

Both. HDR is the reason Gloam exists: the gamma fix operates inside the HDR signal path, and colorimeter corrections install as a native MHC2 profile that the Windows compositor applies persistently, including with HDR enabled. HDR calibrations measure true HDR-range patches at exact PQ wire positions instead of guessing how Windows maps SDR content onto the wire.

SDR displays are fully supported as well, including gamma control, night mode, and full colorimeter calibration.

Which colorimeters work?

Any meter supported by ArgyllCMS, which Gloam downloads automatically and runs as a separate process for the measurement step. The X-Rite i1 Display family (i1 Display Pro, i1 Display Studio, and the Calibrite successors) is the well-trodden path. Spectrophotometers supported by Argyll work too.

Whatever the meter, use a spectral correction matched to your panel type. Gloam's built-in browser searches the DisplayCAL community corrections database and downloads .ccss/.ccmx files directly. On QD-OLED and wide-gamut panels this is not optional: generic corrections can misplace the white point by several dE.

Anything special about QD-OLED?

Two things. First, QD-OLED emission spectra are a pathological case for generic colorimeter corrections; use a QD-OLED .ccss from the in-app corrections browser or your readings will faithfully reproduce your meter's misplaced D65.

Second, if your panel already measures close to target natively (many QD-OLEDs do), prefer the white-point-only correction mode, which Gloam auto-suggests for OLED. Full gamut correction cannot improve what is already inside measurement noise, and verification will reliably come back worse if you try. Warm the panel up before measuring.

How is this different from DisplayCAL or Calman?

Honestly: for SDR desktop calibration, DisplayCAL is mature, excellent, and more configurable, and Calman is the professional standard for TV and mastering workflows. If that is your use case, use them.

Gloam's differentiator is the HDR-native pipeline on Windows 11. Traditional tools apply corrections through the GPU gamma ramp (VCGT) and ICC profiles that the Windows Advanced Color compositor largely ignores or resets when HDR is on. Gloam builds a properly domain-wrapped MHC2 matrix plus PQ-domain tone LUTs, installs them through the Advanced Color association the HDR pipeline actually reads, and verifies the result by measuring through the applied profile, including a PQ tracking sweep in absolute nits. It also stays resident to keep gamma preference, night mode, and the calibration composing cleanly instead of fighting each other.

Is it free? Open source?

Yes and yes. Gloam is MIT licensed and the source is on GitHub. ArgyllCMS, which Gloam invokes as a separate process for measurement, is AGPL v3; Gloam does not link against it.
